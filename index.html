What is recovery rate?

The share of household waste recovered through recycling, organics or glass services — instead of being sent to landfill. A rate of 100% would mean no household waste went to landfill at all.

Why per person?

A large city like Melbourne generates far more total waste than a small rural council — but that doesn't mean its residents waste more. Per-person measures level the playing field for fairer comparison.

Who is this for?

Designed for Australians who want an honest view of how their local council performs on waste recovery and landfill reduction — and how that compares to the rest of Victoria.

Which councils recover the most waste?

The map shows each Local Government Area shaded by its 2023–24 recovery rate — the proportion of household waste diverted from landfill through kerbside recycling, organics, and glass programs. Darker green indicates stronger performance.

The highest-performing councils in 2023–24 are Manningham (75.0%), Alpine (74.9%), Bass Coast (73.2%), and Merri-bek (72.5%). These councils benefit from long-running organics collection programs, high participation rates, and well-funded local waste infrastructure.

At the other end, West Wimmera (13.2%), Hindmarsh (16.0%), and Yarriambiack (19.6%) lag far behind. These are small, sparsely populated councils in Victoria's far west where kerbside organics collection is either limited or absent entirely, and the economics of running recovery services over large distances are challenging.

The geographic pattern is striking: metropolitan Melbourne and its inner suburbs cluster in the mid-to-high range, but performance is not uniform — Melbourne CBD (28.9%) and Yarra (30.9%) underperform relative to their inner-suburban neighbours, suggesting that high-density apartment living reduces participation in kerbside recycling programs.

Where is landfill waste highest per person?

Adjusting for population reveals a dramatically different picture from total waste volumes. Councils that send the most waste to landfill per resident are almost entirely rural — and the gap is enormous.

Buloke tops the list at 376 kg per person, followed by Hindmarsh at 310 kg and Yarriambiack at 282 kg. By comparison, well-performing metropolitan councils such as Boroondara generate just 98 kg of landfill waste per resident — less than one-third of the rural worst performers.

The rural–urban gap is structural, not simply behavioural. Remote councils lack access to the organics processing facilities, materials recovery centres, and glass collection infrastructure that metropolitan councils take for granted. They also contend with lower population density, which makes kerbside services more expensive per household to operate.

It is worth noting that this metric measures landfill, not total waste generation. A council with a large amount of recovered waste can still generate a high volume of total waste — what matters here is specifically the proportion that ends up buried.

How has Victoria-wide household waste changed?

Combining all council records reveals the long-term trajectory of total household waste across Victoria. From 2001–02, waste grew steadily — driven in roughly equal measure by population growth and increasing per-capita consumption — reaching approximately 2.31 million tonnes in 2023–24.

The most notable disruption is the sharp peak during 2020–21, when total waste reached its highest recorded level of around 2.54 million tonnes. This aligns closely with the extended COVID-19 lockdowns, during which Victorians spent far more time at home, increased online shopping, and produced more domestic packaging waste. Hard waste and household goods disposal also surged as residents used lockdown time for home decluttering.

Since then, waste volumes have declined. The 2023–24 figure of 2.31 million tonnes is broadly in line with pre-pandemic levels, suggesting the pandemic spike was temporary rather than a structural shift in household behaviour. Population growth remains the underlying driver of long-term volume increases.

Has recovered waste grown compared with landfill?

Breaking total waste into its landfill and recovered components tells a more encouraging story. Victoria's overall recovery rate has nearly doubled over the period — from 28.8% in 2001–02 to 52.7% in 2023–24 — meaning that more than half of household waste is now diverted from landfill.

The recovered layer in the area chart has expanded from roughly 450,000 tonnes in 2001–02 to over 1.22 million tonnes in 2023–24. Much of this growth is attributable to the progressive rollout of kerbside organics collection, which processes food scraps and garden waste into compost rather than sending it to landfill. Glass separations and improved recycling processing have also contributed.

Meanwhile, the landfill component has declined in recent years — from its 2016–18 peak of around 1.27 million tonnes down to 1.09 million tonnes in 2023–24. This is a meaningful improvement, though it still represents a substantial volume. Achieving further reductions will require both expanding recovery services to underperforming councils and increasing participation rates in councils that already have services in place.

Best performers and where every council sits

Recovery rates across Victoria's 79 councils span a wide range — from just 13% to 75% — reflecting deep structural differences in geography, income, infrastructure investment, and the types of kerbside services councils have chosen to fund. The first chart highlights the top performers; the second plots all 79 councils against the state average to show the full distribution.

The top performers are a mix of inner-metropolitan and coastal councils. What they share is long-running, well-funded organics collection programs and above-average community participation rates. Across the full distribution, roughly half of councils exceed the 52.7% state average — but the spread is wide, and many rural councils in Victoria's northwest still send the majority of their waste to landfill.

Recovery rate over time for selected councils

The heatmap shows how recovery rates have shifted across a selection of Victorian councils from 2001–02 to 2021–22. Each row is a council; each column is a financial year. Darker green cells indicate a higher proportion of waste diverted from landfill in that year.

The most immediate pattern is directional: reading from left (2001–02) to right (recent years), cells become consistently darker across nearly every council. This confirms the state-wide trend of improving recovery rates, driven by incremental expansions of kerbside programs, improved sorting technology at materials recovery facilities, and growing community awareness about recycling.

Some councils show sharp discontinuities — a sudden darkening in a particular year — which typically corresponds to the introduction of kerbside organics collection. Organics programs often add 15–25 percentage points to a council's recovery rate overnight, since food and garden waste makes up a large fraction of household bins.

There are also a few councils with visible dips in specific years, often coinciding with the collapse of the recycling commodities market in 2018–19, when Chinese import restrictions caused many Victorian councils to temporarily reduce or halt kerbside recycling services.

Do bigger councils produce more waste?

Unsurprisingly, councils with larger populations generate more total household waste — the relationship is close to linear. What is more interesting is the colour dimension: recovery rate does not track population size.

Large councils such as Casey and Wyndham — fast-growing outer-suburban growth corridors — generate substantial total waste but achieve only moderate recovery rates. Meanwhile, mid-sized councils like Manningham and Boroondara combine large waste volumes with high recovery rates.

The outliers in the lower-right quadrant — high waste, low recovery — are the most concerning. They represent councils where both the volume problem and the recovery problem coincide, compounding landfill pressure. Population alone explains total waste volume, but council policy, infrastructure investment, and geographic access explain recovery performance. The scatter plot makes visible that these two dimensions are largely independent.

Which councils landfill is the most waste per person?

This bar chart ranks the 20 councils with the highest landfill waste per resident in 2023–24. The figures are striking: Buloke sits at 376 kg per person, meaning the average resident sends nearly four times their own body weight to landfill every year. Hindmarsh (310 kg) and Yarriambiack (282 kg) follow closely behind.

All of the top 20 are rural or regional councils. Many are located in Victoria's northwest — the Wimmera and Mallee regions — where agricultural communities are spread across large distances and kerbside organics collection has not been introduced. These councils often serve populations of fewer than 10,000 people, making the per-capita economics of running recovery services especially challenging.

The contrast with well-performing metropolitan councils is stark. Boroondara generates just 98 kg of landfill waste per resident. Manningham, the top performer on recovery rate, produces around 88 kg. These differences cannot be explained by resident behaviour alone — they reflect decades of differential investment in waste infrastructure between metropolitan and regional Victoria.

How did selected councils change from 2001–02 to 2023–24?

The slope chart plots recovery rates for ten selected councils at two points in time: the start of the data period (2001–02, left) and the most recent year (2023–24, right). Every line slopes upward, confirming that all ten councils improved their recovery rate over the two-decade span.

The gains are substantial. Several councils that began the period below 20% — including Wodonga and Mornington Peninsula — now sit above 60%. The steepest improvements typically coincide with the introduction of kerbside food and garden organics collection, which in many councils was only rolled out in the late 2000s or early 2010s.

The chart also illustrates that starting position matters. Councils that were already performing well in 2001–02 generally maintained their advantage — the ranking of councils has been relatively stable even as all have improved. This suggests that early investment in recycling infrastructure created a durable lead that has been difficult for later adopters to close.
